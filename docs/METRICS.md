# CS2 demo-anticheat thresholds — what the community actually publishes

**Bottom line up front:** The community publishes far less hard numerical data than your tool needs, but enough to fix the specific failure you hit. The 15% verdict on a confirmed wallhacker came from three compounding mistakes that match well-documented anti-patterns: (a) hard-gating HS% at n=10 instead of down-weighting, (b) leaving pre-FOV out of scoring when it is the single best demo-side wallhack signal published anywhere, and (c) treating short-format variance as a reason to disable signals rather than widen confidence intervals. The headline finding from this research is that **Time-to-Damage and Leetify-style "preaim" are the only two metrics with concrete, defensible community numbers; everything else (snap velocity, recoil MAE, nearest-enemy angle, back-killed %) is folk-knowledge or unpublished.** Practitioners working with these signals (CSWatch, yviler, AntiCheatPT, VACnet) do not publish weighted-sum formulas — they either use ML end-to-end or hard rules with repeat-offense thresholds. Your weighted-sum design is genuinely novel public territory.

The rest of this report gives you per-metric numbers with confidence flags, signal-strength rankings, the sample-size and game-mode adjustments you asked for, and a concrete re-calibration recommendation that would have flagged your wallhacker.

**Two findings deserve special emphasis.** First, the **sub-100ms TTD rate is the single highest-signal published metric.** Leetify's public data shows the NA Premier >25k bucket sitting at ~2% while the Asia >25k bucket sits at **57%** — a regional cheater-density signal so strong the community treats it as semi-conclusive evidence of a cheating epidemic in that bucket. Second, **the preaim "red" threshold of ~2.5° comes from multiple Steam community discussions of confirmed cheaters** at 2.18° preaim where commenters note "people with Preaim 2.18 doesn't exist in this world." Your tool computing preaim but not wiring it into the verdict is the biggest single fix available.

## Why your tool gave 15% to a confirmed wallhacker

The community sources point at exactly the failure modes you described. **Hard-gating HS% at n=10 is the wrong fix for small-sample noise.** Binomial confidence intervals widen smoothly as n shrinks; the statistically correct response is to widen the CI on the metric, not to exclude the metric entirely. With Wilson 95% CIs, n=9 with 80% HS% gives a CI of roughly [49%, 94%] — wide but still informative, certainly more informative than "no signal." The practitioner pattern (Lilac, CSWatch, Leetify) is to surface the metric with uncertainty bars and **combine multiple weak signals**, not to gate them off.

**Pre-FOV is the strongest single wallhack signal in your feature set and you weren't using it.** Every wallhack-focused community source — CSWatch's color bands, Steam community profile-review threads, the Witschel/Wressnegger humanized-aimbot paper, the Leetify glossary — treats some form of crosshair-on-unseen-enemy angle as **the** demo-side wallhack tell. Leetify quietly removed it from public display (cswat.ch changelog confirms this), almost certainly because of the AWP/role false-positive issue, but CSWatch still surfaces it via API and treats ≤~2.5° as blatant. Wiring this into your verdict with a strong weight, gated only on minimum-encounters (5+) rather than minimum-kills, would have likely pushed your wallhacker into the 60%+ likelihood range alone.

**The Wingman boost gating at >10 kills inherits the same bug.** Leetify explicitly acknowledges in their Rating Breakdown post that 16-round matches have higher variance than 30-round matches — but their response is to display ratings anyway with the user understanding the variance, not to suppress them. Your Wingman gate is doing the opposite of what the community does.

## High-signal vs low-signal ranking for modern CS2 cheats

Synthesizing across VACnet (Valve, 2018), AntiCheatPT (2025 transformer), XGuardian (2026 GRU-CNN), yviler's LSTM, and CSWatch's operational rules, here is the practitioner consensus on signal strength for catching 2024–2026 cheats:

**High signal.** Pitch/yaw kinematic derivatives in a tight window around each shot — VACnet's "atom" (-0.5s to +0.25s) and XGuardian's velocity+acceleration+angular-change triple are the dominant features across every behavioral system published. **Sub-100ms TTD rate** as a distribution is Leetify's strongest publicly-cited cheater signal. **Preaim/crosshair placement** is CSWatch's primary wallhack lever. **Repeated 1-tick perfect shots that kill** (Lilac's "Autoshoot" with ≥2 occurrences) catches triggerbots. **Mean angular error below the random-spread floor** (~0.2° for AK) is a no-recoil tell — though no community source published distributions.

**Medium signal.** Headshot % normalized to rank baseline. First-shot accuracy and TTK from Leetify's per-rank-bucket benchmarks. Spray accuracy. Aim Rating composites. These work as combination evidence but generate false positives standalone, especially for AWP-heavy players and high-FACEIT-level legit grinders.

**Low signal.** Single sub-100ms TTD events (pre-fires look identical). Raw K/D, ADR, KAST aggregates. Back kills and smoke kills (legit via callouts). Top-fragging as a standalone prior — CSWatch deliberately weights "behavioral anomalies including aim stats" at only 15% of their composite, with ban record (35%) and account legitimacy (25%) dominating. The community consensus is that **top-fragging slightly raises prior only when accompanied by mechanical anomalies**; smurfing is the dominant alternative explanation.

The **adversarial reality check** comes from Witschel & Wressnegger (arXiv 2004.12183): an aimbot deliberately trained to mimic pro demo distributions evades VAC, VACnet, Overwatch, and two open-source ACs. Modern "humanized" LEGIT cheats with FOV cap, smoothing, target-switch delay, and randomization are the population that breaks behavioral detection. Your tool should target rage-to-mid-tier cheats and accept that top-of-market humanized cheats remain unsolved.

## Sample-size gates and the right way to fix them

The single most important re-calibration is conceptual: **down-weight instead of exclude.** Replace your binary gates with a confidence weight roughly equal to min(1, n / n_full), where n_full is the sample size at which the metric reaches stable confidence intervals. A 9-kill match should contribute ~45% of its full weight, not zero. The recommended n_full values, derived from binomial CI math and the closest community-codified analog (the legacy Battlefield CheatDetector plugin's 30-kill/200-kill rule from IOL0ol1/ProconPlugins):

- **HS%**: soft floor n=8 (down-weighted), full weight at n=20, verdict-grade at n=30. Your current 10 is borderline.
- **Snap velocity**: full weight at n=10 snaps. Your 5 is too low — close-range Wingman fights drive false positives.
- **TTD median**: full weight at n=10 events; the sub-100ms *rate* needs n=30+ for stable percentage. Your gate of 3 is far too low and is the second-biggest fix after wiring in preaim.
- **Recoil MAE**: requires at least one full 20-bullet spray; three sprays for stable cross-match. Burst-firers will simply not produce data — handle "no data" as missing rather than clean.
- **Pre-FOV**: 5 encounters minimum, but per-encounter confidence (crosshair within X° of unseen enemy for ≥Y ticks behind opaque geometry) matters more than raw count. This is the metric where small-n with high-confidence-per-event is legitimate.
- **Back-killed %**: full match's deaths only; never standalone verdict.

The combination layer matters more than any single gate. If three independent signals each register ~60th percentile cheater likelihood, the Bayesian-combined posterior is much higher than any single metric's 15%. Your current scoring almost certainly under-combines weak signals.

## Game mode adjustments — Wingman, FACEIT, and ELO buckets

Wingman systematically changes thresholds in three documented ways. Matches are MR8 (max 16 rounds, often 8–12), 2v2, and on smaller maps. Leetify explicitly does not officially support Wingman; CSstats only processes Competitive/Premier. The Leetify Rating Breakdown post directly states 16-round matches have meaningfully higher variance than 30-round matches. The practical implications: **raise close-range snap-velocity thresholds for Wingman maps** (close fights inflate angular velocity legitimately), **don't lower sample-size gates** (the right move is uncertainty bars, not looser thresholds), and **lean harder on pre-FOV and continuous tracking metrics** which are less per-kill-sample-dependent than HS% or TTD.

FACEIT Level 10 players have HS% averaging ~50%, ADR ~92, K/D ~1.24 per fctracker.org — roughly equivalent to Premier 20k–30k. A flat "60% HS% over 30 kills is suspicious" threshold mis-flags top-tier legits routinely. **ELO-bucket your baselines** using Leetify's published Premier-rating buckets (8k, 9k, 10k...20k–25k, 25k+) as the calibration target; their Season 3 update specifically split 20–25k from 25k+ because top-bucket players have ~6 points higher aim rating than mid-bucket. ESEA is essentially deprecated for thresholding purposes; treat it like FACEIT L9+. Competitive (CS2's casual map-pool ranked) sits lower than Premier on skill distribution, so the same absolute HS% is more suspicious there.

## Common false-positive traps

The community has converged on a recurring list of footguns your tool should explicitly handle. **AWP and SSG players** distort everything: kill-based HS% craters (body shots), preaim looks artificially low (pre-aimed angles are the entire role), and snap velocity is low (slow scoped tracking). Always filter the AWP for HS% (Leetify does this) and tag the player's primary weapon distribution before scoring. **Deagle one-tappers** spike short-sample HS% legitimately. **Aimbots trained on chest/stomach** invert the HS% signal — a confirmed pattern on Steam is VAC-banned profiles with very high accuracy and ADR but sub-10% HS%; this should *raise* suspicion when accuracy and HS% diverge, not lower it. **Map knowledge pre-aiming** (Mirage A site connector, Inferno banana, Dust2 long doors) produces low preaim legitimately; per-map baselines would help but no source publishes them. **Smurfing** is the dominant alternative explanation for top-fragging. **Sub-tick demo interpolation** at 64Hz tick rate gives ±0.1–0.3° irreducible noise on angle deltas; this is why CS:GO recoil MAE thresholds don't carry cleanly into CS2.

## What no source publishes that you'll need to compute yourself

Several gaps emerged consistently across all four research streams and are worth flagging because they shape the engineering work ahead. **No source publishes bare P95/P99 cuts as numbers** for any aim metric; Leetify's Public Data Library shows distributions interactively but doesn't tabulate them — worth scraping directly. **No published per-feature weights exist** in any open-source CS2 cheat detector; the practitioners who solve this problem use ML end-to-end (LSTM, transformer, GRU-CNN) and don't expose feature importance as percentages. **No clean-population baseline for strict pre-FOV (through-wall) angle exists**; Leetify's "preaim" is technically first-spotted-to-first-hit, not through-wall. Theoretical baseline from geometry (uniform sphere) is ~90°, but real CS2 pre-aim baselines are much lower due to map-knowledge legit pre-aiming. **No nearest-enemy-angle or back-killed-% distributions are published** despite both being trivially computable from demoinfocs-golang. **No mode-specific (Wingman) baselines are published.** And **no per-map preaim distributions exist**, which is the false-positive control you'd want for production use.

The most valuable concrete starting points for filling these gaps are the **CS2CD dataset on Hugging Face** (795 matches, 90,707 kill-context windows, 18,423 cheater kills, labeled via VAC + manual review — DOI 10.57967/hf/5315) which lets you compute your own P95/P99 cuts, and **Leetify's per-rank-bucket benchmark posts** as calibration anchors. The XGuardian minimal feature set (pitch/yaw → velocity, acceleration, angular change) is the highest-signal-per-engineering-effort feature trio published.
